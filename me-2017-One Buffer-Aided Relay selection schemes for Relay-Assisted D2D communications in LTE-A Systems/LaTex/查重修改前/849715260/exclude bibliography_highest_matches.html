INTRODUCTION To satisfy the increasing demand of local traffic load and provide better user experience, device-to-device (D2D) com- munications have been proposed for cellular network [? ? ? ]. 1D2D communications can be classified into licensed- band D2D and unlicensed-band D2D [? ]. Working on un- licensed bands is commonly subject to uncontrolled interfer- ence. Hence, most of the existing works focused on licensed- band D2D communications, aiming to improve spectrum efficiency and cell-wise data transmission performance by encouraging flexible radio resource reuse and communication mode selection [? ? ? ]. However, 1most of the recent works have focused on direct D2D (one-hop D2D) communications. 1Only using direct D2D mode may limit the benefits brought in by the D2D commu- nications to cellular network, because source and destination D2D-capable UEs (DUEs) may not be able to perform direct D2D communications due to long separation distance and poor channel condition between them [? ]. In such cases, a promising way to completely explore the benefits of D2D communications is to enable BS -controlled D2D transmission through relays, which can substantially extend the range of D2D communications [? ]. Compared to fixed relay [? ], the relay-capable UE 1(RUE) assisted D2D communications may be more flexible due to an increase in UE density. Hence, most works considered the ”relays” in relay-involved D2D communications as RUEs.1This paper focuses on the relay-assisted D2D, which 1works in the way that two DUEs within an BS’s coverage communicate with each other, with the help of a relay-capable D2D UE under BS’s control [? ? ]. 1To develop relay-assisted D2D communications in cellular systems, the most critical issue is to select optimal relay UE for each D2D-capable communication pair in BS scheduling. Relay selection issues have been extensively studied in cooper- ative communications, ad hoc networks, and sensor networks. Most works 2assume that relaying procedure is performed in two consecutive time slots, where in the first slot, the source transmits to a relay and in the second slot, the relay forwards the received data to the destination. This relaying mechanism is referred to as conventional relaying [? ]. Moreover, 1most of the proposed relay selection strategies were designed in physical layer (PHY), the residual energy and buffer state of candidate relay node was basically ignored. 1Furthermore, the scenarios in most of these strategies did not include channel reuse.1In this paper, we treat relay-assisted D2D communication as a supplement to direct D2D communication in cellular systems, both of which should reuse the radio resources of general cellular UEs (CUEs). We propose a joint 2Energy- Aware and Buffer-Aided (EABA) relay selection strategy 2which uses the information about the channel conditions of the links, the residual energies and buffer states of the RUEs to compute a scheduling weight for each link at each time slot. The link with the highest weight is then selected to transmit data instead of conventional relaying. 10The rest of this paper is organized as follows. EABA Strategy 2In order to transfer the packets of a file in a short period of time, Energy-Aware and Buffer-Aided (EABA) 2works based on the following theory based: if a relay has high residual energy 2and many empty spaces in its buffer, it should get a higher chance for reception. On the other hand, if it has a large number of data in its buffer, 2it should have a higher chance for transmitting a packet to the destination. Moreover, if a link has a high SINR, more data will be transmitted at a time slot, 2it should get a higher priority. V. 1CONCLUSION In this paper, we designed an efficient relay selection scheme for Relay-Assisted D2D communication in cellular network. 1Under the assumption that D2D communication reuse the uplink channels of CUEs, we proposed a Energy- Aware and Buffer-Aided (EABA) 1relay selection scheme, which jointly considered end-to-end data rate, RUEs residual energies, and RUEs buffer states. The 1estimation processes of the end-to-end data rate, RUEs residual energies, and RUEs buffer states 1were described.
